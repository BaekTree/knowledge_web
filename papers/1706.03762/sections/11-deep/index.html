Section 11 deeper explanation

Introduction

The introduction first sets up the problem: before the Transformer, the strongest sequence models were recurrent neural networks, especially LSTMs and gated RNNs. These models process tokens one step at a time, carrying a hidden state forward from position t-1 to t. That design is expressive, but it makes training inherently sequential, so one example cannot be parallelized across time steps. This becomes a major bottleneck for long sequences, because the model must wait for earlier positions before computing later ones. The paper notes that prior work improved efficiency with tricks like factorization and conditional computation, but those methods did not remove the core sequential dependency.

The section then motivates attention as the key alternative. Attention lets a model directly connect one position to another, no matter how far apart they are in the sequence, by assigning weights to relevant tokens instead of relying on repeated hidden-state updates. The important point is that attention had already proven useful, but usually as an add-on to recurrent networks. The Transformer’s main design decision is to remove recurrence entirely and build the model purely from attention mechanisms. That changes the computation graph from a chain into something much more parallelizable, which is why the authors expect faster training and better scalability.

This section also foreshadows the paper’s technical structure. After stating the motivation, the next step is to define how the Transformer actually works: how attention is computed, how information is represented without recurrence, and how the model handles both input and output sequences. In other words, the introduction makes the claim that global dependency modeling does not require recurrence, and the rest of the paper is the derivation of a concrete architecture that proves it.
