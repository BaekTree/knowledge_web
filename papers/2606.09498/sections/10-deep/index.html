Self-Harness: Harnesses That Improve Themselves
arxiv:2606.09498

Section 10 deeper explanation

Experiments

This section sets up the core experiment: instead of changing the language model, Self-Harness tries to improve the *harness* around it, meaning the scaffolding that schedules actions, tracks state, validates outputs, and decides when to stop or retry. The benchmark is Terminal-Bench-2.0, which measures how well an agent can operate in containerized terminal environments. The important technical idea is that performance gains can come from better orchestration logic, not just a stronger model. So the authors keep the model fixed and let Self-Harness search for small, bounded edits to a DeepAgent-based baseline harness. The design is careful about avoiding overfitting. Self-Harness does not freely rewrite the system; it proposes candidate changes, validates them against held-in execution evidence, and then promotes only those that also pass held-out regression gates. “Held-in” evidence is the data used to support a change during development, while “held-out” gates are separate checks meant to catch regressions on unseen cases. This setup reflects a practical engineering tradeoff: the harness should become more capable, but it must remain stable and not accidentally break behaviors that already worked. Bounded edits also matter because they make the optimization space smaller and the results easier to attribute to the harness rather than to uncontrolled redesign. This section is the experimental bridge from method to evidence. It defines the baseline, the benchmark, and the validation protocol that will be used to judge whether self-modification actually helps. The next step in the paper is therefore to show what kinds of improvements these harness edits produce, across multiple model backends, and whether the gains hold under the regression checks rather than disappearing as benchmark-specific overfitting.
