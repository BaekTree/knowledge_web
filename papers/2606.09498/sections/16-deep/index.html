Main Results

Self-Harness demonstrates substantial performance improvements across three distinct model backends, with gains that vary in magnitude but remain consistent in direction. For MiniMax M2.5, the promoted harness achieves a held-in improvement from 43.0% to 50.0% (7 percentage points, 16% relative gain) and a held-out improvement from 40.5% to 61.9% (21.4 percentage points, 53% relative gain). Qwen3.5 shows the most dramatic relative improvements—held-in performance nearly doubles from 15.1% to 36.0% (138% relative gain)—while held-out performance improves from 23.8% to 38.1% (60% relative gain). GLM-5 achieves more modest but consistent gains of 20% held-in and 33% held-out improvements. These results validate that harness-level modifications can produce meaningful performance gains independent of model selection, tool availability, computational budget, or evaluation infrastructure.

The held-out performance improvements are particularly significant because they directly address the generalization challenge at the core of the Self-Harness approach. A harness that improves held-in performance—the data used to generate edit proposals—is expected to overfit to those specific failure cases. The fact that all three models show substantial held-out gains (53%, 60%, and 33% relative improvements) indicates that the promoted edits target reusable execution patterns rather than memorizing case-specific fixes. This generalization pattern validates the central design principle: by operating at the harness level and filtering edits through a regression gate, the method discovers broadly applicable modifications to how the system orchestrates tool use and reasoning, not brittle patches for individual failures.

The regression gate mechanism prevents a critical failure mode in iterative improvement: degradation on one evaluation split in pursuit of gains on another. By ensuring that no promoted harness worsens performance on either held-in or held-out data, the system guarantees that each iteration represents a strict improvement to the overall execution strategy. This constraint is computationally disciplined—it means each proposed edit must pass a two-part verification before promotion, eliminating false improvements that would appear beneficial during proposal generation but fail under broader evaluation. The consistency of improvements across splits suggests that the edits address fundamental issues in execution strategy rather than exploiting artifacts of the training or evaluation distribution.

These results show how systematic harness improvement can scale across different model architectures and training approaches. Neither the magnitude of initial weakness (Qwen3.5's 15.1% baseline versus GLM-5's 47.7%) nor differences in model design prevent Self-Harness from finding beneficial edits. The relative gains are large enough to be practically relevant—a 53% improvement on held-out performance for MiniMax M2.5 represents the difference between a system that fails on roughly three-fifths of problems and one that succeeds on nearly two-thirds—while remaining robust enough that no promoted harness becomes worse at its job.
