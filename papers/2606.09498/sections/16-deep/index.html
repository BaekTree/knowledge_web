Self-Harness: Harnesses That Improve Themselves
arxiv:2606.09498

Section 16 deeper explanation

Main Results

This section introduces the paper’s core evaluation setup for Self-Harness: a “harness” is the execution wrapper around a model, including how it calls tools, structures prompts, manages budgets, and decides when to stop or retry. The authors compare a baseline harness with a “promoted” Self-Harness after editing. They evaluate on Terminal-Bench-2.0 using two splits: held-in, which covers failures used to generate proposal evidence, and held-out, which tests whether the edits generalize beyond the examples that motivated them. Performance is reported as Pass (%), along with both absolute gains in percentage points and relative gains computed from the initial score, so readers can see both the raw improvement and how large that improvement is relative to the starting point. The design choice to keep the model backend, tool set, budget, benchmark environment, and evaluator fixed is important: it isolates the effect of the harness itself. In other words, the paper is asking whether better orchestration can improve outcomes even when the underlying model does not change. The results show that harness edits can do exactly that. Across MiniMax M2.5, Qwen3.5, and GLM-5, the promoted harness improves or at least preserves performance on both splits, and the held-out gains are especially important because they indicate the edits are not merely overfitting to the failures that inspired them. The relative improvement formula makes the strongest gains easier to compare across models with very different baselines. This section sets up the next step in the paper by establishing that harness changes are worth studying as a first-class optimization target. Once the authors show that promotion can reliably improve generalization without harming other settings, the natural next question is what kinds of edits caused the gains and how the system decides which proposed edits are safe to promote.
