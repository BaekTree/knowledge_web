Section 16 deeper explanation

Main Results

This section reports the core empirical result of Self-Harness: once a candidate harness edit is promoted, it does not just help on the failure cases that motivated the edit, but also improves or at least preserves benchmark performance on a separate held-out split. The metric being tracked is Terminal-Bench-2.0 Pass (%), which measures whether the system successfully completes benchmark tasks. The important design choice here is that the paper evaluates the harness itself while holding the model backend, tool set, budget, benchmark environment, and evaluator fixed, so any change in Pass can be attributed to the harness logic rather than to model capacity or execution resources.

The numbers show a consistent pattern across all three backends. MiniMax M2.5 rises from 43.0 to 50.0 on held-in and from 40.5 to 61.9 on held-out. Qwen3.5 increases from 15.1 to 36.0 on held-in and from 23.8 to 38.1 on held-out. GLM-5 moves from 47.7 to 57.0 on held-in and from 42.9 to 57.1 on held-out. The absolute gains are already meaningful, but the relative gains are especially informative when the starting point is low: a 21-point increase from 15.1 to 36.0 is a much larger proportional change than the same raw increase at a higher baseline. That is why the paper reports both absolute percentage-point gains and relative gains.

The relative gain formula, \((Self\text{-}Harness - Initial)/Initial\), expresses improvement as a fraction of the original score. This normalization matters because the three backends start from very different baselines, so raw deltas alone would obscure how large a change is in context. For Qwen3.5, for example, the held-in gain of 20.9 points corresponds to a 138% relative improvement because the original score was only 15.1. For MiniMax M2.5 and GLM-5, the relative gains are smaller but still substantial, showing that the effect is not limited to weak starting systems. In all cases, the promoted harness improves or preserves both splits, which is the key regression-control property the self-harnessing process is designed to enforce.

Conceptually, this result supports the paper’s broader claim that useful harness edits are reusable execution mechanisms rather than ad hoc fixes for one trace. That interpretation aligns with the harness evolution and retained-edits discussion, where accepted changes are meant to survive promotion only if they generalize beyond the specific failure traces that generated them. It also matches the trace-level analysis of accepted edits, which is used to show that the promoted changes address systematic coordination or execution issues rather than memorizing individual cases. In the conclusion, this becomes the main evidence that self-improving harnesses can raise benchmark performance without changing the underlying model, which is the central technical contribution of the paper.
