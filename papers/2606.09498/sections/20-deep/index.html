Model inference services

This section is about the paper’s inference infrastructure, not the modeling method itself. The main idea is that different LLMs were served in different ways depending on what was practical: MiniMax M2.5 was used through MiniMax’s hosted API, GLM-5 through OpenRouter, and Qwen3.5-35B-A3B through a local deployment. “Hosted inference” means the model runs on someone else’s managed service, so the researchers send prompts and receive completions without running the model weights themselves. By contrast, local deployment means the team controlled the serving stack and hardware directly, which is useful when they need predictable throughput, lower operational dependence, or custom runtime settings.

The design choice here is mostly about balancing convenience, reliability, and control. Hosted services are the fastest way to access proprietary or externally hosted models, and they avoid the overhead of building and maintaining a serving stack. The paper notes concrete service entry points, such as the MiniMax token plan and the OpenRouter GLM-5 endpoint, because those are the exact access paths used during the experiments. That matters for reproducibility: model access is not just “which model,” but also “through which service and configuration,” since the service can affect latency, rate limits, and sometimes even the exact version available. The “Accessed on 2026/05” note is part of that provenance.

The local Qwen deployment uses four NVIDIA H200 GPUs and an internal image derived from the public SGLang Docker image. SGLang is an inference serving framework designed for high-throughput LLM execution, so using it signals that the authors wanted a production-style runtime rather than a toy script. The Docker image choice captures the environment deterministically: the public base image gives a known serving stack, and the internal derivative lets the team customize it for their cluster without losing the benefits of a standard upstream version. Four H200s indicates the model was large enough, or the throughput target high enough, that a single GPU was not sufficient. In practice, this kind of setup is chosen to stabilize batching, reduce per-request overhead, and make generation speed more predictable.

The broader intuition is that the paper is standardizing model access before evaluating self-improvement. If the harness is supposed to compare or optimize model behavior, then inference variability needs to be controlled as much as possible. This section therefore sets the experimental substrate: it tells the reader which models were remote, which were self-hosted, and what runtime they used. The next step in the paper can then focus on harness behavior and performance, with the serving layer treated as fixed infrastructure rather than a hidden source of variation.
