Model backend

The study evaluates eight different large language model backends spanning four major providers: Anthropic Claude (Opus 4.6 and Sonnet 4.6), Google Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview, Qwen, GLM, Moonshot Kimi, OpenAI GPT, and DeepSeek (Table 5). This diverse selection of models across different provider families serves as the basis for the factorial evaluation of harness effects. By including models from competing providers rather than focusing solely on a single vendor's offerings, the researchers can assess whether harness configuration effects are model-specific phenomena or generalizable patterns that hold across different base LLM architectures and training approaches.

A critical methodological decision is the treatment of Codex as a separate evaluation target rather than integrating it into the main configurable harness–model factorial matrix. Codex represents a qualitatively different kind of system: it is a model-bound coding agent with its own fixed model configuration and specialized execution interface, rather than a general harness that can be paired with arbitrary backend models. By reporting Codex separately and using it as a practical reference point, the researchers isolate the effect of harness architecture from the effect of model-specific capabilities. This separation allows them to ask distinct questions: how much of Codex's performance comes from its specialized design, versus how much comes from its particular underlying model?

The category-level analysis of harness dependence (Appendix C) reveals that harness effects are not uniformly distributed across task types. Computing variance across harness-level averages within each workflow category, the authors discover that tasks in Data, BI & Finance Analytics, Workspace/Tool Use, and Software Engineering show substantially higher variance across harness configurations—up to 0.0155 variance units—compared to Office & Business Communication tasks, which show only 0.0020 variance. This heterogeneity is not random: it correlates directly with task structure. Categories where harness effects are large tend to involve structured data manipulation, multi-step tool sequencing, workspace state tracking, and maintaining consistency between intermediate artifacts. In contrast, language-centric tasks that rely primarily on natural language generation exhibit lower sensitivity to harness configuration choices.

This distribution of harness dependence carries important implications for agent design. The finding suggests that harness effects emerge most strongly when task success requires maintaining tight alignment between an agent's reasoning process, its tool invocations, the evolving workspace state, and verifiable output artifacts. These are precisely the conditions where the harness's structural properties—how it frames observations, sequences tool calls, manages memory, and validates outputs—matter most. Conversely, tasks that succeed through language generation alone are more robust to harness variation, possibly because language quality alone provides sufficient signal for task completion. The results thus point toward a design principle: harnesses should receive particular engineering attention in domains where structured reasoning and state management are critical to task success.
